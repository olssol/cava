A Batch-Effect Adjusted Simon's Two-Stage Design for Cancer Vaccine Phase II Clinical Studies

1 Abstract

In the development of cancer vaccines, phase II clinical studies examine the efficacy of a vaccine at the optimal dosage determined in the corresponding phase I studies in order to screen out vaccines with poor activities. Because of its simplicity and ethical considerations, Simon's two-stage design and its variations are popular tools for designing cancer phase II clinical studies. Outcomes from different patients are typically assumed independent from each other in Simon's two-stage design. For cancer vaccine studies, immune responses from multiple patients are usually measured as batches. In the presence of batch effects, the outcomes are correlated with each other. Thus, the independence assumption does not hold. In this paper, we conduct simulation studies to explore the impact of batch effects on phase II studies with Simon's two-stage design. Furthermore, we propose an adjusted Simon's two-stage design that accounts for batch effects and evaluate its performance by simulation studies.

2 Background

Batch effect has long been recognized as a thorny issue for assays \citep{youden1972enduring}. It can be confounded with the outcomes of interest, cause significant bias in the measurements, and lead to serious concerns about the validity of the findings. Although approaches have been proposed to address batch effects through improved experimental design or statistical adjustments, These proposals are primarily suitable for high-dimensional assays such as microarrays, bead chips and next-generation sequencing, where there are enough data to detect and remove batch effects via sophisticated modeling approaches \citep{alter2000singular,benito2004adjustment,johnson2007adjusting,leek2010tackling}. In low-dimensional settings such as clinical trial design and analysis, batch effect adjustment is often neglected. To the best of our knowledge, research addressing and mitigating the impact of batch effects on clinical trials is very limited.

In this paper, we focus on evaluating the impact of batch effects on a specific clinical trial design: Simon's two-stage design \citep{simon1989optimal}. Simon's two-stage design allows the investigators to conduct straightforward interim analysis at the end of the first stage and to stop the study early for futility. Because of its simplicity and ethical considerations, Simon's two-stage design is widely adopted in phase II cancer clinical studies. In Simon's two-stage design, outcomes from different patients are assumed independent from each other. This independence assumption, however, does not hold in the presence of batch effects. \cite{sargent1999sample} proposed an approximation method, hereinafter referred to as the Sargent's method, to adjust Simon's two-stage design for per-tumor analysis when each patient may have multiple tumors. We propose a simulation-based batch-effect adjusted Simon's two-stage design to take into account batch effects at study design. We also conduct numerical studies to evaluate the performance of the proposal, compare it with the Sargent's method, and demonstrate it by a therapeutic HPV vaccine trial. Finally, we have developed statistical software to implement the proposed design, and a brief introduction to the software is given in the paper.
